Meanderings of an Unquiet Mind

Overview
A series of poetry-driven web experiments
Audience
Poets, creatives and sleepless humans
Keywords
Web design
Poetry
Experimental

Problem

Insomnia and sleeplessness are often seen as being the result of disease or disorder, and thus having very negative connotations. The potential for creating a sense of beauty from the darkness is lacking in our society, and presents an opportunity to include those that may feel excluded though these labels. For some the night is the time where they come alive, and come up with their greatest ideas.

Brief

Create an experimental website that showcases an interpretation of insomnia without looking at it as a disease, but rather a state that everyone has experienced at some time in their life. A series of five web experiments should talk to each other to tell a fragmented narrative. The website should push the bounds of what is capable within the web browser, using experimental implementations of code. Change perceptions of what is possible.

Solution

Meanderings of an Unquiet Mind as the title suggests, is an exploration of a state in which the mind functions when the world is quiet. The dark of night is a time where for some the world comes alive, and they are unable to enter into the realm of sleep. The experimental website explores this limbo state through the use of short poems that are supplemented by visuals that abstract five stages of sleeplessness.

The website was created using p5.js a JavaScript-based visual coding language based off the Processing project. The complex simulations are all randomly generated through complex mathematical operations. The experience is a hybrid of experimental code, poetry and music, blending to create the murky haze of late-night unquiet minds.

Experiment 1   —   Lune


The first experiment introduces the user to the experience, helping to get them into a peaceful space of mind. This is also reflected in the use of a simple poem and the stark visuals.

The circle represents the cycle of laying awake, that seems to never end. The visual opens the experience as though the cycle begins here.

The poem tells the same story; a cycle keeping sleep at bay.

Experiment 2   —   Triolet


The second experiment introduces the user to interactivity, while inviting them to calm down further.

The visual allows the user to manipulate the waves on the screen reacting to the position of the cursor. While not implicitly stated, the user can find a point on the screen where there is no movement – calmness.

The poem explores the search for peace of mind, and questions what is preventing this. The poem’s style reflects the repetition theme of the previous experiment.

Experiment 3   —   Nonet


The third experiment is all about contrast. It is not interactive, but rather a visual experience.

The poem uses the subtractive form of the disappearing nonet, which loses a syllable on every consecutive line. The poem itself explores the way in which thoughts may seem to hold us captive as they flow through our mind.

The poem is reflected in the visual narrative by swirling lines that move randomly and form complex patterns and waves. As they overflow into the black box of the poems, they change colour, slowly obscuring parts of the poem.

Experiment 4   —   Haiku

The penultimate experiment is once again interactive. The overlay explains the interaction, as the canvas is presented as blank.

The user can click anywhere on the screen, and a line of the poem will spawn continuously from the cursor position. As the user clicks again, another line of the poem appears.

The poem is read between any three points on the screen, as it is a haiku. While it is written to this form, it can be read in whichever order the viewer likes. The poem links to the movement in the visual, telling of ideas floating like falling leaves.

Experiment 5   —   Gogyohka

The final experiment is the slowest of all. The user is allowed to read the poem in their own time, as the music builds slowly. This experiment becomes more beautiful as time progresses.

The poem is the darkest of the five, and handles with the final dread of not falling asleep. The style is loose and wild like one may be in the final stages of sleeplessness.

The visual is akin to ink droplets running down the screen. They seem to harbour a sense of dread and sadness, as they begin to smother the poem. By hovering over the poem, the user may be able to glimpse fragments of it once more.
